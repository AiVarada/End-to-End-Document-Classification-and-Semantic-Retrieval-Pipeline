GUN CONTROL LEGISLATION — SENATE COMMITTEE DEBATE

The proposed background check expansion bill has generated intense debate in the Senate 
Judiciary Committee this week. Supporters of the legislation argue that closing the 
so-called gun show loophole is a common sense measure that would prevent firearms from 
reaching criminals and individuals with a history of mental illness.

Senator Williams, one of the bill's primary sponsors, stated during Tuesday's hearing 
that the current system allows unlicensed private sellers to transfer firearms without 
conducting any background check whatsoever. She cited data suggesting that approximately 
22 percent of all firearm transfers in states without universal background check laws 
occur without any screening of the buyer.

Opponents of the bill argue that it represents an unconstitutional infringement on 
Second Amendment rights. The National Rifle Association and several other gun rights 
organizations have mobilized their membership to contact senators and express opposition. 
Their primary argument is that the legislation would burden law abiding gun owners 
while doing little to prevent determined criminals from obtaining weapons through 
illegal channels.

Senator Thompson, who leads the opposition on the committee, argued that the Heller 
decision clearly established an individual right to keep and bear arms that cannot 
be infringed by such regulations. He introduced an amendment that would limit the 
background check requirement to licensed dealers only, which was rejected by the 
committee majority on a party line vote of eleven to nine.

The bill is expected to face a difficult path to a full Senate floor vote. Several 
moderate senators from both parties have indicated they are undecided and are waiting 
to see the results of a Congressional Budget Office analysis of implementation costs. 
The White House has signaled that the President would sign the legislation if it reaches 
his desk in its current form.

A similar measure passed the House of Representatives last month by a margin of 
240 to 190, largely along party lines with eight Republicans joining all Democrats 
in support of the bill.
